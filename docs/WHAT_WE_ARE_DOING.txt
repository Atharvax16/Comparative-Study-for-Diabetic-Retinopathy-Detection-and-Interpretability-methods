================================================================================
WHAT WE ARE DOING AND WHY
Thesis: Robust and Explainable AI for Diabetic Retinopathy Classification
================================================================================


THE PROBLEM
───────────

Diabetic retinopathy (DR) is the leading cause of preventable blindness in
working-age adults. Over 100 million people worldwide are at risk. The
solution is early detection through regular screening of the retina using
fundus photographs. But there aren't enough ophthalmologists — especially
in low-resource settings where the disease burden is highest.

Deep learning has stepped in. Models like ResNet, EfficientNet, and Vision
Transformers can look at a fundus image and grade the severity of DR on a
5-point scale (No DR, Mild, Moderate, Severe, Proliferative) with accuracy
that matches or exceeds human specialists. On clean, well-captured images,
published results hit 84-89% accuracy on the APTOS 2019 benchmark.

But here's the problem nobody talks about: those results are measured on
perfect images. In the real world — a rural clinic in India, a mobile
screening van in sub-Saharan Africa, a GP's office with a handheld retinal
camera — images come out blurry (camera shake, wrong focus), too dark or
too bright (improper lighting), or grainy (cheap sensor, low light). Studies
show 15-30% of real-world screening images are too poor for reliable diagnosis.

What happens to that 88% accuracy when the image is blurry? Nobody knows,
because nobody has systematically tested it.

And there's a deeper problem. Modern clinical AI doesn't just give a
diagnosis — it shows WHY it made that decision, through heatmaps
(Grad-CAM, SHAP) that highlight which parts of the image the model looked
at. A doctor sees the heatmap pointing at a cluster of microaneurysms and
thinks "okay, the AI is looking at the right thing, I trust this diagnosis."

But what happens to that heatmap when the image is degraded? Does the
model still look at the lesion, or does it start looking at blur artifacts,
noise patterns, brightness gradients? If the heatmap shifts to clinically
irrelevant regions while the diagnosis stays correct, the doctor has false
confidence in an AI that's guessing for the wrong reasons.

This is the question nobody has asked. We ask it.


================================================================================
WHAT WE ARE ACTUALLY DOING
================================================================================

We built a five-phase experimental pipeline that systematically answers
four research questions. Here's each phase and what it does:


PHASE 1: DATA ENGINEERING
─────────────────────────

We take the APTOS 2019 dataset (2,930 fundus images with DR severity labels)
and synthetically degrade every test image in controlled, reproducible ways:

  - Gaussian blur at 3 severity levels (sigma = 1.5, 3.0, 6.0)
    → simulates camera defocus / shaky hands
  
  - Exposure shift at 3 severity levels (gain = 0.7/1.3, 0.5/1.6, 0.3/2.0)
    → simulates wrong lighting / overexposure / underexposure
  
  - Gaussian noise at 3 severity levels (std = 0.03, 0.08, 0.15)
    → simulates cheap sensor / low-light capture

This gives us 9 degraded variants per test image (3 types × 3 levels).
The degradation is the experiment, not a training trick. We train on clean
images with standard augmentation. We evaluate on degraded images to
expose how models fail.

Why synthetic and not real degradation? Because synthetic degradation is
controlled and reproducible. We know exactly what we applied (sigma=6.0
blur), so we can measure exactly how much accuracy drops and how much the
explanation shifts. With real degradation, you can't isolate variables.


PHASE 2: MODEL BENCHMARKING UNDER STRESS
─────────────────────────────────────────

We train three architectures representing two fundamentally different
approaches to image understanding:

  CNNs (local pattern matching):
    - ResNet50: the workhorse CNN, processes images through hierarchical
      stacks of small (3×3) convolutional filters
    - EfficientNet-B3: a more efficient CNN with squeeze-and-excitation
      blocks that recalibrate channel-wise features

  Vision Transformer (global attention):
    - ViT-Base: divides the image into 16×16 patches, computes attention
      across ALL patches simultaneously, allowing each region to
      incorporate information from every other region

We train all three on clean APTOS images and then evaluate them on every
degradation condition. This answers RQ1.

What we found:
  - On clean images: ViT leads at 84.77%, with ResNet at 75.68% and
    EfficientNet at 76.59%. A modest gap.
  - Under severe blur: ViT retains 56.82%. ResNet drops to 47.27%.
    EfficientNet collapses to 7.95%. A massive gap.
  - Under severe noise: ViT retains 61.82%. ResNet drops to 49.77%.
    EfficientNet collapses to 20.00%.

The robustness gap between ViT and EfficientNet under blur is 49 percentage
points — nearly half the entire accuracy scale. This gap is completely
invisible if you only test on clean images.

WHY ViT is more robust: self-attention processes 16×16 patches (256 pixels
each), smoothing out per-pixel noise. It can also "route around" corrupted
patches by attending more heavily to informative ones. CNNs process through
tiny 3×3 filters that are devastated by pixel-level corruption.

WHY EfficientNet collapses worse than ResNet: EfficientNet's compound
scaling makes it more parameter-efficient but also more specialised to
the exact input distribution it was trained on. ResNet's simpler, deeper
architecture has more redundancy.


PHASE 3: EXPLAINABILITY UNDER DEGRADATION
─────────────────────────────────────────

This is the novel core of the thesis. We run three explainability methods
on every (model, image, degradation condition) combination:

  Grad-CAM (for CNNs):
    Hooks into the last convolutional layer, computes gradient-weighted
    activation maps. Produces a coarse (7×7 or 14×14) heatmap showing
    which spatial regions activated most strongly for the predicted class.
    Fast, widely used, but low spatial resolution.

  SHAP — SHapley Additive exPlanations (for all models):
    Perturbs different image regions and measures how the prediction
    changes. Assigns a Shapley value (from game theory) to each region,
    quantifying its contribution. Model-agnostic, theoretically grounded,
    fine-grained. Works on both CNNs and ViTs.

  Attention Rollout (for ViT only):
    Aggregates attention weights across all transformer layers by
    multiplying attention matrices. Shows where the model attends.
    Native to transformers, no hooks needed.

For each heatmap, we compute three metrics:

  Stability (SSIM):
    How similar is the degraded-image heatmap to the clean-image heatmap?
    1.0 = identical explanations, 0.0 = completely different.
    This is our key novel metric. It measures "explanation drift."

  Insertion AUC:
    Progressively reveal pixels in order of heatmap importance. How quickly
    does the prediction confidence rise? Higher = better explanation.
    Tests whether the heatmap correctly identifies important pixels.

  Deletion AUC:
    Progressively remove pixels in order of heatmap importance. How quickly
    does the prediction confidence drop? Lower = better explanation.
    Tests the same thing from the opposite direction.

What we found:
  - Grad-CAM stability on ResNet drops to 0.19 under heavy noise. The
    heatmap is almost unrecognisable compared to clean.
  - SHAP stability drops even further — to 0.002 and even negative values
    (-0.086), meaning the explanation is ANTI-CORRELATED with clean.
    The model is actively looking at opposite regions.
  - ViT attention maps are more stable (0.80 under noise) but with
    lower class-discriminative precision.
  - Insertion AUC remains moderate even when stability is zero. This means
    SHAP accurately reflects what the model is doing (faithful), but what
    the model is doing is no longer clinically meaningful (drifted).

The key insight: a model can produce the correct diagnosis on a degraded
image while its explanation points at completely wrong regions. The
diagnosis is right by accident. The explanation provides false confidence.
This is "explanation drift" — a previously unreported failure mode.

SHAP is more sensitive to degradation than Grad-CAM. Grad-CAM's apparent
stability is partly an artifact of its coarse 7×7 resolution, which
spatially smooths away the noise-level detail. SHAP, operating at
pixel/superpixel resolution, honestly reveals the chaotic fragmentation
of the model's attention. For clinical deployment, SHAP is the more
honest method — it fails loudly rather than hiding the failure.


PHASE 4: GENAI-BASED IMAGE RESTORATION
───────────────────────────────────────

If degradation causes accuracy loss AND explanation drift, can we fix
the image before diagnosing? We compare three restoration approaches:

  CLAHE (traditional baseline):
    Contrast Limited Adaptive Histogram Equalisation. A non-learned
    method that improves local contrast. Simple, fast, no training
    required. Answers: "how far does a non-AI method get?"

  A-ESRGAN (GAN-based):
    Attention-Enhanced Super-Resolution GAN. Uses a deep residual
    network with attention discriminators. Pretrained on natural images.
    Answers: "how does a general-purpose GAN perform on fundus images?"

  Conditional DDPM with Pathology Preservation (our main contribution):
    A standard denoising diffusion probabilistic model (DDPM) with three
    specific design choices for clinical safety:

    1. RESIDUAL ARCHITECTURE: The model predicts the degradation residual,
       not the clean image. Output = degraded_input + model_prediction.
       Pathological features (lesions, microaneurysms, vessel patterns)
       flow directly through the skip connection. The model only learns
       to remove degradation, not to generate anatomy.

    2. PATHOLOGY-AWARE PERCEPTUAL LOSS: Instead of using VGG features
       (trained on cats and cars), we use our own trained EfficientNet-B3
       DR classifier's features as the perceptual loss backbone. This
       forces the diffusion model to preserve features that the DR
       classifier considers diagnostically important.

    3. INFERENCE-TIME CLAMPING: At every reverse denoising step, the
       predicted clean image is anchored toward the degraded input:
         x_pred = (1 - λ) × model_output + λ × degraded_input
       This prevents the model from hallucinating features that don't
       exist in the original image.

    4. HALLUCINATION DETECTION: After restoration, we compare:
       - DR grade before vs after (did the diagnosis change?)
       - Classifier activation maps before vs after (did attention shift
         to a previously inactive region?)
       - Pixel-level deviation (how much did the image actually change?)
       If the grade changes or activations shift to new regions, the
       restoration is flagged as potentially hallucinated.

  Why diffusion over GANs? The professor specifically directed us here.
  Diffusion models have been shown to produce higher-fidelity restoration
  than GANs with fewer artifacts, because the iterative denoising process
  allows fine-grained control at each step. GANs generate in a single
  forward pass with no intermediate correction opportunity. The iterative
  nature also allows us to inject the pathology preservation clamp at
  every step, not just at the end.

  Why not Stable Diffusion? Stable Diffusion is a text-to-image generation
  model (860M parameters). It generates images from text prompts. We need
  an image-to-image restoration model that's lightweight enough for
  clinical deployment. Our conditional DDPM is purpose-built for this task.

After restoration, we re-evaluate all three classifiers on the enhanced
images. The comparison becomes:
  raw degraded accuracy → CLAHE accuracy → A-ESRGAN accuracy → DDPM accuracy

But accuracy recovery is only half the story. We also re-run SHAP and
Grad-CAM on the restored images and compare heatmaps against both the
degraded and clean baselines. This is Phase 4's key contribution:
does restoration recover the EXPLANATION, not just the accuracy?

A GAN might hallucinate textures that boost classifier confidence while
shifting the heatmap to regions that don't correspond to real pathology.
The accuracy recovers, but the explanation is now driven by artifacts.
Our hallucination detection catches this.


PHASE 5: QUALITY-AWARE ENSEMBLE
───────────────────────────────

We tie everything together into a practical system:

  1. A quality classifier (ResNet-18 trained on the EyeQ dataset) assesses
     the quality of each incoming fundus image: good, usable, or reject.

  2. Based on predicted quality, the image is routed through the optimal
     pipeline:
       Good    → no enhancement, use ViT-Base (best clean accuracy)
       Usable  → CLAHE enhancement, use ViT-Base
       Reject  → DDPM restoration, use ViT-Base, run hallucination check

  3. A clinical trust score combines:
       trust = 0.5 × prediction_confidence + 0.5 × explanation_fidelity
     Where explanation fidelity = insertion AUC of the SHAP/Grad-CAM heatmap.

     A prediction with 90% confidence but garbage explanation (insertion
     0.2) gets trust 0.55. A prediction with 80% confidence and faithful
     explanation (insertion 0.8) gets trust 0.80. The second one is more
     clinically trustworthy.

  4. Images with trust score below a threshold are flagged for human
     review rather than auto-diagnosed.

This is the practical clinical contribution: a system that doesn't just
diagnose, but knows when to trust itself and when to ask for help.

THE FOUR RESEARCH QUESTIONS
================================================================================

RQ1: How do Vision Transformers compare to CNNs in DR classification
     performance across varying levels of synthetic image degradation?
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

What it asks: When image quality drops, which architecture family
degrades more gracefully — local-filter CNNs or global-attention ViTs?

What we found: ViT is dramatically more robust. Under severe noise,
ViT retains 61.82% accuracy while EfficientNet collapses to 20%.
This 42-point robustness gap is invisible in clean-image benchmarks.
ResNet sits in between at 49.77%, failing more gracefully than
EfficientNet but far worse than ViT.

Why it matters: If you're deploying DR screening in a setting where
image quality is variable (which is most real-world settings), the
choice of architecture is not just a performance decision — it's a
patient safety decision. A ViT-based system will catch roughly 3×
more disease cases than an EfficientNet system when images are noisy.

Phase: 2 (model benchmarking under stress)


RQ2: To what extent does the fidelity and stability of explainability
     methods (Grad-CAM, SHAP, Attention Rollout) degrade under image
     quality conditions?
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

What it asks: When the image degrades, does the model's explanation
stay reliable? Or can a model be right for the wrong reasons?

What we found: Explanations degrade FASTER than accuracy. Grad-CAM
stability on ResNet drops to 0.19 under noise-high (the model still
gets ~50% accuracy, but its explanation is unrecognisable). SHAP is
even more sensitive, dropping to near-zero or negative stability,
revealing that the model's fine-grained attention has completely
restructured to track noise artifacts instead of pathology.

The critical insight: moderate insertion AUC coexists with zero
stability. SHAP faithfully tracks what the model is doing (the
explanation is accurate), but what the model is doing is clinically
wrong (it's looking at noise, not lesions). A faithful explanation
of a drifted model is accurate but dangerous.

Why it matters: No published paper measures this. The assumption
that "if the model is right, the explanation is trustworthy" is
wrong. Our stability metric catches a failure mode that accuracy
alone misses — and this failure mode is exactly what would mislead
a clinician relying on AI-generated heatmaps.

Phase: 3 (XAI benchmark under degradation)


RQ3: Can GenAI-based image enhancement significantly recover both
     diagnostic accuracy AND XAI localisation precision compared
     to traditional methods?
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

What it asks: If we fix the image before diagnosing, do we recover
not just the accuracy but also the explanation? And can we do it
without hallucinating fake pathology?

What we're building: A conditional DDPM with residual architecture
(pathological features flow through skip connections), pathology-
aware perceptual loss (using our own DR classifier features, not
VGG), inference-time clamping (anchoring output to degraded input),
and explicit hallucination detection.

Why "both accuracy and XAI" matters: A GAN could restore a blurry
image and the classifier's accuracy might jump from 47% to 70%.
But if the GAN hallucinated textures that the classifier uses for
its prediction, the heatmap now points at fake features. The
accuracy "recovered" but the explanation is driven by artifacts.
This is more dangerous than no restoration, because it creates
false confidence. Our post-restoration XAI verification catches
this.

Why it matters: This is the first work to evaluate restoration
not just on accuracy recovery but on explanation recovery. Every
published fundus enhancement paper measures PSNR/SSIM or
downstream accuracy. None check whether the explanation is
trustworthy after restoration.

Phase: 4 (GenAI enhancement + post-restoration XAI verification)


RQ4: How can a quality-aware ensemble framework dynamically select
     the optimal enhancement–model–XAI combination to maximise
     clinical trust?
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

What it asks: Can we build a system that automatically detects
image quality, routes to the right restoration/model/XAI pipeline,
and produces a trust score that a clinician can act on?

What we're building: A three-stage pipeline:
  1. Quality assessment (trained on EyeQ, applied to any image)
  2. Conditional routing (no enhancement / CLAHE / DDPM)
  3. Trust scoring (confidence × explanation fidelity)

Why it matters: The real-world product is not "a model that
classifies DR." It's "a system that classifies DR, knows when
the image is too degraded to trust, fixes it if possible, and
tells the doctor whether to trust the result." That's what
RQ4 builds.

Phase: 5 (quality-aware ensemble with trust scoring)


================================================================================
WHAT MAKES THIS NOVEL — THE ONE-SENTENCE VERSION
================================================================================

No published work connects degradation, explainability, restoration, and
post-restoration explainability verification into a single framework.

Papers that classify DR stop at clean accuracy.
Papers that study XAI show heatmaps on clean images.
Papers that enhance fundus images measure PSNR.
Nobody asks: "When the image degrades, does the explanation drift? And
when we restore the image, does the explanation come back?"

We ask that question, measure it quantitatively, and build a system
that detects and prevents the failure mode.


================================================================================
THE THESIS IN ONE PARAGRAPH
================================================================================

AI diagnoses diabetic retinopathy with high accuracy on clean images, but
real-world screening produces degraded images that cause both diagnostic
accuracy and the AI's visual explanations to fail. We show that explanations
degrade faster than accuracy — a model can diagnose correctly while its
heatmap points at noise artifacts instead of lesions, giving clinicians
false confidence. Vision Transformers are dramatically more robust than
CNNs under degradation (62% vs 20% under severe noise), a gap invisible
in clean benchmarks. We build a pathology-aware conditional diffusion model
that restores degraded images while preserving diagnostic features through
residual architecture, classifier-guided perceptual loss, and explicit
hallucination detection. Critically, we verify that restoration recovers
the explanation, not just the accuracy — the first work to do so. A
quality-aware ensemble ties it all together, routing each image through
the optimal pipeline and producing a clinical trust score that integrates
diagnostic confidence with explanation reliability.
